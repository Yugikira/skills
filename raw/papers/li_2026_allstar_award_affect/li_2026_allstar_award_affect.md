## ABSTRACT

This paper examines the effect of the All-Star award on the performance of Chinese financial analysts. Leveraging unique voting data from 2007 to 2016 and a regression discontinuity design (RDD), we find that the All-Star award significantly enhances recipients' fundamental analysis. Awarded analysts issue more accurate earnings forecasts, and their stock recommendations convey greater information content for firms with higher information asymmetry. RDD results also indicate that award recipients gain increased resources and greater flexibility in reallocating time and effort. Post award, analysts concentrate on fewer industries, cover more firms within each industry, issue forecasts more frequently, expand their teams, and conduct more site visits. Surveys of analysts and institutional investors corroborate these findings, highlighting increases in site visits and roadshows following the award. Overall, the results suggest that the All-Star award boosts analyst performance by fostering more concentrated coverage and improving access to both internal and external resources.

## RÉSUMÉ

Le présent article se penche sur l'effet du prix All-Star (prix d'excellence) sur le rendement des analystes financiers chinois. À l'aide de données de vote uniques couvrant la période de 2007 à 2016 et d'une régression sur discontinuité (RSD), nous établissons que le prix All-Star améliore considérablement les analyses fondamentales des lauréats. Les analystes qui ont reçu ce prix font des prévisions de résultats plus précises, et leurs recommandations sur les titres comportent une plus grande quantité d'information pour les sociétés où l'asymétrie de l'information est importante. Les résultats de la RSD indiquent également que les lauréats du prix bénéficient de plus vastes ressources et d'une plus grande souplesse aux fins de la redistribution de leur temps et de leurs efforts. Après avoir obtenu le prix, les analystes récompensés se concentrent sur un moins grand nombre de secteurs d'activités, s'attardent à davantage de sociétés dans chaque secteur, formulent plus fréquemment des prévisions, agrandissent leur équipe et effectuent plus de visites sur place. Des sondages effectués auprès d'analystes et d'investisseurs institutionnels corroborent ces résultats et mettent en évidence l'augmentation des visites sur place et des tournées de présentation par les lauréats à la suite de l'obtention du prix. Dans l'ensemble, le prix All-Star améliore le rendement des analystes en favorisant une couverture plus ciblée et en améliorant l'accès aux ressources internes et externes.

## 1 | Introduction

Awards are designed to provide signals of recognition and distinction (Frey and Gallus 2017). In the financial profession, the All-Star award is an important recognition for financial analysts (Lowengard 2017). A large literature suggests that All-Star analysts exert a significant influence on the financial market. Their research and services are valued across the sell-side, buy-side, and corporate sectors (e.g., Gleason and Lee 2003; Hong and Kubik 2003; Groysberg et al. 2011; Brown et al. 2015; Guan et al. 2019). Despite extensive research on the determinants of All-Star status, however, we know little about whether the All-Star award itself affects analysts' behavior and performance. In this study, we use a proprietary voting data set and employ a regression discontinuity design (RDD) to test the causal effects of the All-Star award on analysts' performance. Specifically, we examine whether the award alters analysts' behavior and enhances their performance in fundamental analysis and resource allocation in the context of the Chinese institutional setting. We conduct follow-up surveys of analysts and institutional investors to further explore the mechanisms underlying the findings from the RDD.

Prior literature in economics and management offers two competing views on the effect of awards on subsequent performance. On one hand, awards are intended to recognize outstanding performance. Recipients may be motivated to maintain or further improve their performance to uphold the prestige associated with the title (Neckermann et al. 2014; Gallus and Frey 2016; Gallus 2017). In the analyst profession, receiving the All-Star award is directly tied to economic and social benefits. For example, Chinese brokerage firms widely incorporate All-Star ranking as a core performance metric for equity analysts. Additionally, winning the All-Star award elevates analysts' social status, granting them access to more resources both within and outside their brokerage houses, which can further improve their performance. This halo effect is likely more pronounced in China, where social recognition through titles and awards carries particular importance (Fisman et al. 2018; Li, Xu, et al. 2023). Analysts recognized as All-Stars can leverage the prestige of the award to gain greater autonomy in firm selection and access to enhanced internal resources, such as larger research budgets, more capable team members, and privileged communication with managers of covered firms. $ ^{1} $ The recurring nature of the award further enhances the motivation to continue to pursue it. Taken together, these arguments suggest that the All-Star award may have a positive impact on analyst performance.

On the other hand, recent studies find that awards can sometimes reduce an individual's motivation to exert effort. Recipients may feel content with their achievements and become less driven in their subsequent work (Gallus and Frey 2016, 2017; Robinson et al. 2021). The status associated with an award may also become a distraction, diverting attention from core responsibilities (Wade et al. 2006; Malmendier and Tate 2009; Huang et al. 2020). Once they attain All-Star status, analysts may receive more invitations to participate in activities that are peripheral to their research. Thus, it is an empirical question whether the award ultimately enhances or diminishes analysts' performance.

The endogenous nature of the All-Star status presents an empirical challenge in assessing its effect on recipients' subsequent performance. For example, analysts with better performance are more likely to be selected as All-Stars. and the award may correlate with unobservable analyst characteristics or activities that consistently influence performance. To draw stronger causal inferences, we leverage a proprietary voting data set from New Fortune, an influential Chinese financial magazine that has administered the All-Star awards since 2003. Each year, All-Star awards are granted to the top three analysts in industries with up to 20 analysts, and to the top five analysts in industries with more than 20 analysts. This novel data set enables us to identify analysts located just above or below the award threshold and to apply the RDD to estimate causal effects. The key assumption underlying the RDD is that analysts near the award threshold do not have precise control over their voting outcomes. This condition is satisfied in the context of All-Star voting due to the large number of participants and the ex ante uncertainty surrounding the exact vote share threshold required to win. As a result, assignment to the All-Star award for analysts around the threshold can be considered locally random (Lee and Lemieux 2010). We validate this assumption by confirming that there are no significant discontinuities in the analysts' pre-event performance or behavior around the threshold. Our sample covers the period from 2007 to 2016.

After merging the analyst rankings with analyst forecasts and other financial information, we obtain a final sample consisting of 13,163 analyst earnings forecasts and 495 unique participating analysts. Using regression discontinuity analyses, we find that All-Star award recipients exhibit significantly better forecast accuracy than non-recipients in the year following the award. Specifically, All-Star recipients perform 7.8% more accurately in forecasting annual earnings than their non-recipient counterparts, which is approximately 14.2% of the sample mean. We then examine the information content of stock recommendations issued by award recipients versus non-recipients. We find that the market reactions to stock recommendations from award recipients are significantly stronger than the reactions to recommendations from non-recipients in the subsample of smaller and less liquid firms, where information asymmetry is high. The evidence suggests that the All-Star award has a significantly positive effect on analysts' performance in fundamental analysis.

Next, we investigate whether the All-Star award affects the resource allocation of analysts. Since All-Star award voting is based on industries, award recipients may focus more on specific industries to improve their industry knowledge, which has been found to be the most important factor in earning the award (Bradshaw 2012; Kadan et al. 2012; Li et al. 2020). $ ^{2} $ Consistent with this notion, we find that after receiving the award, All-Star analysts issue more earnings forecasts per industry, cover fewer industries, and cover more firms within each industry, while the total number of firms covered and the total number of forecasts issued remain unchanged. These results support the idea that All-Star analysts reallocate their efforts after receiving the award, concentrating more intensively on specific industries, whereas non-winners receive no comparable feedback regarding where to focus and therefore leave their coverage unchanged. We further examine whether the award affects analysts' access to resources. We find that All-Star analysts tend to lead larger teams and conduct more corporate site visits following the award. Overall, these results support the view that receiving the All-Star award facilitates both the reallocation of effort and improved access to resources.

We conduct additional tests to further examine the impact of All-Star awards on several common indicators of analysts' behavior and performance in the literature. First, we find that after receiving the award, All-Star recipients exhibit marginally less bias in their earnings forecasts than non-recipients. This supports our main finding that winning the award enhances the performance of All-Star analysts and suggests that the improvement in forecast accuracy may stem partly from reduced pressure to issue biased forecasts. Second, while we observe no significant difference in the profitability of buy recommendations over the  $ [+2, +20] $ and  $ [+2, +60] $ trading-day windows between Star and Non-Star analysts, we find that in a subsample where no other star analysts make recommendations during the same window, buy recommendations from award recipients are more profitable than those from non-recipients. Third, we find that both first-time and repeat award recipients demonstrate better forecast accuracy than non-recipients, which suggests that the All-Star award has a persistent positive effect on analyst performance. Finally, we perform several robustness tests to further support our main findings. We conduct a falsification analysis using alternative thresholds that mimic the actual award cutoff and find no significant discontinuities at these placebo thresholds. To address the concern about the inclusion of analysts whose rankings are far from the award threshold, we reestimate our models using a symmetrical window around the cutoff. We further test the robustness of our results by relaxing our sample selection restrictions and including all of the top 15 analysts or all forecasts (instead of only initial forecasts) in the analysis. Across all specifications, our main conclusion holds: All-Star recipients outperform non-recipients following the award. Our results are also robust to controlling for brokerage firm size, number of firms and industries followed, earnings forecast frequency, forecast horizon, firm size, and market-to-book ratio, as well as using a constant sample of firms, that is, those covered by the same analysts both before and after receiving the award.
To supplement our archival findings, we conduct two surveys of analysts and institutional investors who participated in All-Star analyst voting in 2020. We received responses from 102 analysts and 385 institutional investors. A substantial percentage of respondents believe that award recipients conduct more site visits and receive greater internal support from their brokerage houses after they win the award. The field evidence aligns with our archival results and suggests that the All-Star award facilitates analysts' access to internal and external resources. Overall, these analyses suggest that the All-Star award enhances analyst performance by encouraging greater focus and effort concentration and increasing access to resources.

Our study makes several contributions. First, it contributes to the literature on financial analysts. Complementing prior studies that largely examine the determinants of All-Star awards, our study fills a void in the literature by showing that these awards have important economic consequences for the profession. By estimating the discontinuities in analysts' industry coverage and forecast frequency, we provide evidence that All-Stars can mobilize their resources and become more specialized in specific industries, which satisfies the demand of institutional investors. Collectively, our findings suggest that the All-Star award is not merely a popularity contest; rather, it facilitates improved equity research within the sell-side analyst profession. More broadly, our study complements the literature on how rankings and recognition can influence the behavior of finance professionals (Kirchler et al. 2018).

Second, our paper contributes to the management literature by deepening the understanding of the effect of awards. Thus far, few studies have successfully isolated the causal effect of awards on post-award performance. We establish a causal link between the All-Star award and subsequent analyst performance. Using RDD to sharpen the identification of the treatment effect, we find that the award significantly improves analysts' future performance. Our study distinguishes the two directions of causal relationships between awards and performance. The RDD approach helps us exclude the possibility that the All-Star award simply reflects the past activity of particularly excellent analysts. Given the prevalence of awards across many industries, understanding their causal impact on recipient behavior has broad implications. Our findings may be generalizable to other professions where similarly prestigious awards are given.

Third, our study enhances our understanding of the brokerage industry and the behavior of equity analysts in China. While extensive empirical research has examined the incentives of equity analysts in the United States, we have limited insight into the incentives driving Chinese equity analysts, which may be quite different given the unique features of Chinese capital markets (Lu et al. 2023). Consequently, our findings highlight mechanisms that may not fully generalize to the US context. Specifically, our findings reveal that the All-Star award creates a halo effect in terms of resource allocation within Chinese brokerage firms. In addition, our surveys of analysts and institutional investors provide valuable field evidence, offering insights into potential mechanisms that are difficult to capture through archival data. $ ^{3} $

## 2 | Institutional Background, Literature, and Hypothesis

### 2.1 | Equity Research and All-Star Analysts in China

Since the establishment of the Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock Exchanges in 1990, the brokerage industry in China has experienced rapid growth. By the end of 2021, China had 140 brokerage firms, 41 of which were publicly listed. Total revenue from brokerage services, proprietary trading, and underwriting rose from US 1.61 billion in 2005 to US 61.3 billion in 2021. $ ^{4} $ The demand for equity research has also grown significantly over the past three decades, along with the number of financial analysts.

In China, however, analysts are prohibited from receiving compensation tied to proprietary trading or underwriting activities. In 2011, the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) introduced Regulation No. 28, known as the Preliminary Provisions on the Release of Securities Research Reports. This regulation mandates that brokerage firms sever connections and restrict communications between their equity research divisions and their proprietary trading and investment banking divisions. The objective is to mitigate conflicts of interest in the issuance of research reports and other related services. Due to such regulations, funding for analysts comes primarily from brokerage commissions, which serve as indicators of the quality of analysts' research.

As in other countries, institutional investors in China are not members of the stock exchange and must execute trades through brokerage firms. To increase trading speed, they often conduct trades through multiple brokerage firms. To allocate commissions among these firms, institutional investors commonly have their lead traders evaluate the quality of research across brokerages and distribute commissions based on those assessments. The top-ranked brokerage firms receive the largest share of commissions. In some cases, brokerage firms may dismiss equity analysts who contribute the least to generating brokerage commissions. Consequently, equity analysts are often highly engaged with fund managers, actively promoting their research service and helping their firms secure higher commission revenue.

New Fortune, an influential Chinese financial magazine, has been organizing a series of annual voting events in China, including elections to select All-Star analysts, Golden board secretaries, and the best investment banks. The All-Star analyst voting, which commenced in 2003, has the longest history. Like Institutional Investor in the United States, New Fortune sends ballots to buy-side fund managers in China, who vote for top analysts based on the quality of their research and service. $ ^{5} $ In 2016, the last year of our sample period, over 1500 financial analysts from 50 brokers and more than 4000 fund managers from 1100 investment institutions participated in the voting. The voters represent the majority of investment institutions in China. The voting consistently achieves a remarkably high response rate that approaches 100% each year. The subsequent award ceremony extends invitations to analysts and executives from participating institutions. This ceremony spans half a day and is accompanied by a full-day research forum, which attracts substantial attention from both the media and private sectors. It is considered one of the most significant events in China's financial industry. The process and outcome are audited by Deloitte, one of the world's Big 4 accounting firms.

The final ranking of All-Star voting in China is based on the sum of weighted votes. Each fund manager's vote for the analyst is weighted according to the net asset value of the fund under their management (Gu et al. 2019). Hence, larger funds are more influential in the voting process. In industries with up to 20 analysts, the three analysts with the highest total votes are recognized as stars. In industries with more than 20 analysts, five analysts are recognized as stars. According to New Fortune's annual survey of institutional investors in 2020, the top-ranked features in the All-Star ranking are industry knowledge and logic in research reports, integrity and professionalism, ability to network with firms and professional associations, timely feedback, performance of past earnings forecasts, and stock recommendations. These are quite similar to the features ranked by US institutional investors.

Chinese brokerage firms widely use the New Fortune ranking as one of the key performance indicators for analysts, and they integrate the ranking into their assessment and incentive compensation systems. Some brokerage firms even give the ranking the largest weight when they evaluate analysts. For example, the base salary for the top All-Star Chinese analyst was around US 1 million a few years ago, which was extremely high relative to the average income level in China (Li et al. 2020). Moreover, receiving an All-Star award not only yields significant individual benefits for analysts but also triggers a substantial influx of brokerage commissions for the firms they are associated with. Such prestigious titles help firms generate business and enhance their reputation. The number of All-Star analysts within a brokerage firm is often indicative of the firm's research quality. Many smaller or mid-sized brokerage firms experience rapid growth by strategically recruiting All-Star analysts from their competitors. In some cases, the acquisition of All-Star analysts can lead to brokerage commission increases exceeding 400%. $ ^{6} $ Therefore, outside career opportunities sharply increase for All-Star award recipients.

### 2.2 | Literature on All-Star Analysts

Prior studies document that All-Star analysts play a crucial role and have a significant impact on the financial markets outside of China. For example, Guan et al. (2019) suggest that the departure of All-Star analysts represents a brain drain for their former employers and even for the sell-side equity research industry. Do and Zhang (2020) find that hiring star analysts improves the performance of incumbent analysts. Meanwhile, All-Stars have a strong impact on the stock market. Gleason and Lee (2003) document that All-Stars' forecasts elicit a stronger immediate price response but a less pronounced subsequent price drift, which suggests that the price-adjustment process is faster and more complete for these analysts. Moreover, the opinions of All-Stars can even have a direct impact on corporate decisions. For example, Harrison et al. (2018) suggest that downgrades by All-Star analysts are positively related to director exit.

Many prior studies investigate the determinants of All-Star awards. For example, analysts who have good social skills, have specific connections, make forecasts on firms in industries related to their past experience, work in teams, or have moderate levels of work-life balance are more likely to become All-Star analysts (L. Fang and Huang 2017; Bradley, Gokkaya, et al. 2017; Bradley et al. 2020; B. Fang and Hope 2021; Hope et al. 2021; Li, Lin, et al. 2023). In China, mutual fund managers are more likely to vote for their connected analysts (Gu et al. 2019). Female analysts are less likely to be elected as All-Stars, but the likelihood increases with their facial attractiveness (Li et al. 2020).
Even though ample evidence documents the determinants of All-Star titles and the importance of these titles in the capital market, an important unanswered question is whether the All-Star award itself has an impact on analyst performance. A related line of literature studies the association between All-Star analysts and their performance and finds mixed evidence. For example, Stickel (1992) and Malloy (2005) show that star analysts have better forecast accuracy. Similarly, L. Fang and Yasuda (2014) and Kucheev et al. (2017) find that All-Star analysts outperform non-stars in their stock recommendations. On the other hand, some studies do not find evidence that All-Star analysts have superior performance. For example, Emery and Li (2009) consider All-Star rankings a popularity contest. They argue that All-Star rankings are largely based on analysts' visibility rather than their quality, which means that the performance of All-Star award recipients is not significantly different from that of non-recipients. Overall, prior studies focus on whether All-Star analysts perform better than other analysts or whether All-Star rankings pick up top performers, not on the effect of the All-Star award on analysts' performance. Moreover, these studies largely use US-based data.

There are two directions of causality between award and performance. The first runs from top performance to receiving the award, while the second runs from receiving the award to improved subsequent performance. Simply documenting that All-Star analysts outperform other analysts, or the opposite, does not disentangle the two causal links—whether better analysts win the award or whether the award recipients perform better. Our study emphasizes the second causal link and differs from prior research by investigating the effect of All-Star awards, rather than determining whether All-Star recipients are gifted analysts.

### 2.3 | Hypothesis Development

Prior studies suggest that awards benefit recipients in several ways. First, receiving an award motivates individuals to work harder and exert effort in their post-award job. Winning awards generates a positive image, encouraging individuals to exert effort to gain more recognition and visibility (Ariely et al. 2009; Kosfeld and Neckermann 2011). Chan et al. (2014) find that winning awards encourages scholars to concentrate on undertaking research and being more productive during the post-award period. Meng (2015) theoretically posits that star analysts have reputational incentives to provide high-quality service. With an incentive to win the award again, All-Star analysts are likely to enrich their industry knowledge by focusing on specific industries and to issue more frequent and timely forecasts, since industry knowledge and timely feedback are among the features institutional investors value most (Groysberg et al. 2011; Bradshaw 2012; Kadan et al. 2012; Brown et al. 2015). All-Star titles give the recipients more control over their tasks and more flexibility to reallocate their efforts among different tasks.

Second, winning awards enhances the social status and bargaining power of recipients, allowing them better access to resources and thereby facilitating better performance (Neckermann et al. 2014; Frey and Gallus 2017). This effect is particularly notable in China. Chinese culture and society value awards across various sectors (Fisman et al. 2018). Receiving a prestigious award creates a halo effect, providing recipients with access to diverse resources from both their organizations and external channels. $ ^{7} $ The New Fortune All-Star analysts ranking is the most important award in equity research (Gu et al. 2019; Li et al. 2020). Since the acquisition of All-Star analysts can lead to remarkable increases in brokerage commissions, external career opportunities sharply increase for All-Star analysts. To retain their current stars, companies are likely to further increase their support and grant extra power to All-Star analysts. Thus, All-Star titles can unlock a greater pool of resources within their brokerage firms. For example, All-Star analysts have more junior staff to handle routine responsibilities, focus on more challenging service activities, and collaborate more with colleagues in other sectors. Their requests for larger budgets or additional resources (e.g., corporate site visits) are more likely to get approved by the brokerage firm. Meanwhile, holding the title enables analysts to have better communication with managers in their covered companies (Mayew 2008; Green et al. 2014; Soltes 2014). All-Star analysts may also increase the size of their industry network and thus receive more value-relevant information through their connections (Bonner et al. 2007). This explanation is consistent with the Matthew Effect, an accumulated advantage whereby success breeds success (Merton 1973).

Overall, we expect All-Star awards to have a positive causal effect on analyst performance. Summarizing the preceding discussions, we form the following hypothesis (in alternative form).

Hypothesis. All-Star award recipients just above the award threshold perform better in fundamental analysis than non-recipients just below the award threshold during the post-award period.
### 3.1 | Sample Selection

Table 1 summarizes the sample selection. We start with 1527 candidates who are ranked in the top 15 for each industry in the star analyst voting between 2007 and 2016. New Fortune provided us with exclusive access to the information on these candidates, including analysts' names, associated brokerage firms, industry sectors, rankings, and weighted votes. We manually identify the analysts in the China Stock Market & Accounting Research (CSMAR) database according to their names and work experience. After excluding observations without forecast EPS, actual EPS, and stock price issued between the earnings announcement date of year t and the end of June of year  $ t+1 $, we are left with 1082 analysts. $ ^{8} $ For each year, we calculate a percentage of votes for each analyst using the votes they receive scaled by the total number of votes that the top 15 analysts receive for each industry. We use the percentage of votes that the analysts who barely win the All-Star award receive as the threshold. For industries with three (five) stars, we use the percentage of votes that the third-place (fifth-place) analysts receive as the threshold. To avoid the extrapolation bias in global polynomial regressions, we include All-Star award recipients and non-recipients whose voting shares are within an 8% symmetric window around the cutoff (i.e., 4% above or below the award cutoff). $ ^{9} $ Our final sample consists of 13,163 analyst-year-firm forecasts representing 495 unique analysts for the period 2007 to 2016.

### 3.2 | Key Variables on Fundamental Performance

Our primary performance measure for an analyst's fundamental analysis is earnings forecast error (AFE), which is defined as the absolute value of the analyst's initial earnings forecast for firm j minus firm j's actual earnings per share in year t, scaled by the stock price at the beginning of the year. Following Clement and Tse (2005), we standardize AFE to range from 0 to 1 to control for firm-year effects. The standardized AFE for analyst i following firm j in year t is calculated as  $ [AFE_{i,j,t} - \min(AFE_{j,t})]/[\max(AFE_{j,t}) - \min(AFE_{j,t})] $, where  $ \max(AFE_{j,t}) $ and  $ \min(AFE_{j,t}) $ denote the largest and smallest earnings forecast errors, respectively, of all of the analysts following firm j in year t. The standardized AFE is calculated based on all available one-year-ahead earnings forecasts for firm j in year t. Our main tests are constructed based on this primary measure, as the accuracy of earnings forecast signals the quality of analysts' research.

### 3.3 | Research Design

Our analysis uses an RDD comparing the analysts around the All-Star award threshold. $ ^{[11]} $ The running variable is the distance from the vote share cutoff in each industry centered at zero around the All-Star threshold, which is constructed as follows:

 $$ D i s t a n c e_{i}=V o t e S h a r e_{i}-C u t o f f\_{V} o t e S h a r e_{i}, $$ 

where  $ \text{VoteShare}_i $ is the vote share for each analyst using the votes that the analyst receives scaled by the total number of votes that the top 15 analysts receive for each industry.  $ \text{Cutoff\_VoteShare}_i $ is the vote share cutoff for All-Star awards for each industry (i.e., the vote share of the analyst at the cutoff ranking of the All-Star award). In this way, for All-Star award recipients (non-recipients),  $ \text{Distance}_i $ is greater than or equal to (less than) zero.

In our main test, we start with global polynomial regressions by estimating the following model using our entire sample (Cunat et al. 2012; Bradley, Kim, et al. 2017; Chen et al. 2019):

 $$ A F E_{i,j,t+1}=\alpha+\beta\times S t a r_{i,t}+\sum_{n=1}^{N}\theta_{n}\times D i s t a n c e_{i,t}^{n}+\sum_{n=1}^{N}\varphi_{n}\times S t a r_{i,t}\times D i s t a n c e_{i,t}^{n}+\varepsilon_{i,j,t}, $$ 

where  $ AFE_{i,j,t+1} $ is the forecast error of the analyst's first forecast issued between the earnings announcement date of year t and the end of June of year  $ t+1 $. Star denotes All-Star analyst award status, an indicator variable set to one if an analyst is ranked in the top three or five by New Fortune in year t, and zero otherwise. n is an integer ranging from 1 to N, where N is the order of the flexible polynomial functions.  $ \beta $ is the coefficient of interest, representing the estimated treatment effect of All-Star award assignment.

Next, we follow Calonico et al. (2014) and estimate nonparametric local linear regressions with a triangular kernel and the MSE-optimal (minimizing the mean squared error) bandwidth on the left and right sides of the award threshold (Khan et al. 2017; Chen et al. 2019). The advantage of nonparametric local linear regressions is that they focus on the estimation only near the cutoff and are less sensitive to observations far from the cutoff.

We further examine the information content of stock recommendations as a supplementary measure of analysts' fundamental performance. Specifically, we analyze market responses to analysts' stock recommendations as measured by 3-day size-adjusted abnormal returns. As in our analysis on earnings forecasts accuracy, we first validate the assumption on RDD application holds. Next, we show the effect of the All-Star award on the information content of analyst recommendations for our full sample and partitioned samples. Panel A of Table 5 documents the results for the full sample. The coefficient estimates on Star are positive but insignificant, suggesting that the information content of stock recommendations from All-Star award recipients is no different from that of recommendations from non-recipients. $ ^{14,15} $ We test three possible explanations for the null results.

First, there may be information leakage prior to the public issuance of stock recommendations. For example, important clients may receive these recommendations earlier than the general public (Desai and Jain 1995). To address this possibility, we compare stock returns over the 10- and 25-day periods preceding recommendations issued by award recipients and non-recipients. We find no significant differences, which suggests that pre-announcement leakage is unlikely to be a major driver.

Second, the Chinese stock market has a unique investor structure that may contribute to the insignificant results for the full sample. Unlike other major stock markets, which are primarily dominated by institutional investors, retail investors account for approximately 81% of the daily trading volume in China (Jones et al. 2025) $ ^{16} $. Jones et al. (2025) also find that smaller retail investors—who represent 77% of all retail investors—tend to misinterpret contemporaneous public earnings news. $ ^{17} $ In China, analysts typically issue stock recommendations in the same reports as their earnings forecasts. Retail investors may struggle to process earnings information effectively, potentially introducing noise into the market. Thus, analyst recommendations may be more important for firms with greater information asymmetry.

We classify firms into two groups based on the degree of information asymmetry, which is proxied by firm size and trading liquidity. The marginal value of analyst reports is higher when the firm's information environment is weaker.

## 5 | Effects on Analysts' Behavior and Access to Resources

### 5.1 | Effect of the All-Star Award on Industry Coverage, Firm Coverage, and Forecast Frequency

We next explore how the All-Star award affects analysts' behaviors, which may explain the underlying mechanism affecting analysts' fundamental performance. Prior studies suggest that award recipients change their effort allocation after they receive the award (Malmendier and Tate 2009; Frey and Gallus 2017). All-Star analysts may reallocate their efforts to focus more on specific industries or firms. They may also reallocate efforts to issue more frequent and timely forecasts. We test this conjecture by investigating the post-award industry coverage, firm coverage, and forecast frequency of award recipients and non-recipients around the threshold.
Specifically, we measure industry coverage (IndCovrg) as the natural logarithm of the number of industries followed by the analyst between the ceremony dates of years  $ t $ and  $ t+1 $. Firm coverage (NFirmInd) is measured as the number of firms per industry followed by the analyst between the ceremony dates of years  $ t $ and  $ t+1 $. Forecast frequency (FreqInd) is the number of earnings forecasts per industry issued by the analyst between the earnings announcement date of year  $ t $ and the ceremony date of year  $ t+1 $. Our descriptive analysis shows that, on average, All-Star (non-star) analysts cover 5.13 (4.78) industries, generate reports on 4.17 (3.84) firms per industry, and issue 9.76 (9.19) forecasts per industry after the All-Star award has been presented.

Again, we first validate the RDD assumption and find that the All-Star award recipients and non-recipients exhibit no significant discontinuities at the award threshold in pre-award industry coverage, firm coverage, and forecast frequency. We next test whether the All-Star award affects the post-award industry coverage, firm coverage, and forecast frequency of award recipients and non-recipients. We estimate nonparametric local linear regressions with a triangular kernel and a variety of bandwidths, including optimal bandwidth and fixed bandwidths of 0.01, 0.015, and 0.02. In Panel A (B) of Table 6, we find that the coefficients on Star are negative (positive) and statistically significant at the 5% level or better, suggesting that All-Star award recipients cover fewer industries (more firms per industry) after they receive the award. In terms of economic significance, the treatment effect of the All-Star award on industry coverage is -31.6%. In an untabulated test, we examine the total number of firms covered by each analyst and find no significant differences between award winners and non-winners during the post-award period. The results are consistent with the argument that All-Star analysts reallocate their efforts after they receive the award and tend to increase their concentration on specific industries to enhance their industry knowledge. $ ^{18} $ In Panel C, the coefficient estimates on Star are positive and significant, suggesting that All-Star analysts issue more earnings forecasts per industry. Overall, these results are consistent with the interpretation that performance is enhanced by a reallocation of efforts.

### 6.3 | Robustness Checks

We conduct a series of robustness checks and discuss the results in this subsection. First, we reestimate local linear regressions for repeat and first-time recipients. Table 9, Panel A, shows that the coefficient estimates on Star are negative and significant (p-value <0.01) for both subsamples. The persistent positive effect on the performance of recipients is consistent with the conjecture that analysts have incentives to continue to work hard, and that receiving an All-Star award helps them secure resources. Second, we impose an additional sample restriction, requiring that the analyst be near the award threshold in terms of ranking. Table 9, Panel B, shows results consistent with the main finding—award recipients exhibit higher forecast accuracy. $ ^{[21]} $ Third, we replicate analyses using all forecasts issued prior to the award ceremony and report results in Panel C of Table 9. $ ^{[22]} $ Fourth, although controls are unnecessary in RDD (Lee and Lemieux 2010), we estimate OLS regressions including controls for analyst and firm characteristics. Table 9, Panel D shows that across various bandwidths, the award effect remains significant (p <0.05). Finally, we conduct falsification tests using alternative cutoff points ( $ \pm1\% $ and  $ \pm2\% $) where All-Star status does not change. Untabulated analyses show that the coefficient estimates on Star are insignificant in all cases, suggesting that the improved forecast accuracy of All-Star analysts is driven by the awards. To address potential selection bias, we repeat the analysis using a constant sample of firms, that is, those firms covered by the same analysts both before and after winning the award. Untabulated results again confirm that recipients maintain higher accuracy post-award.

### 7.1 | Survey Methodology

We developed survey questions and New Fortune incorporated them into its annual surveys of analysts and institutional investors who would participate in 2020's All-Star analyst voting. In the analyst survey, we asked analysts whether the nature and time allocation of their work would change after they won the award. We also asked which work item would increase or decrease the most in terms of the working hours allocated. In the survey sent to institutional investors, we asked whether the research service provided by analysts with whom they are familiar would change after the analysts won the award. Similarly, for each research service item, there were three options: increase, decrease, and no obvious change. The complete questions appear in Appendix 2.

New Fortune created an online link and sent the survey invitations to the mobile phones of approximately 1000 analysts and 2700 institutional investors. We received 102 valid responses from analysts and 385 from institutional investors. The response rates are comparable to those of other analyst surveys in previous literature (e.g., Brown et al. (2015) report a response rate of 10.9% for their analyst survey). In our analyst sample, there are 47 All-Star analysts in 2020, 19 runners-up, and 36 others. Their profiles suggest that the respondents are high-quality analysts.

### 7.2 | Survey Results

Table 10 shows that 71.7% of respondents indicate that the number of site visits would increase, supporting our inference that analysts gain more access to external resources after winning the award. Of respondents, 91.2% think that the number of roadshows would increase, which means that analysts would get more opportunities to communicate with customers. 52.6% and 58.2% of respondents indicate that internal service and training would increase, respectively, implying that analysts can obtain more internal resources and support from their brokerage firms. Additionally, 83.7% and 56.0% of respondents believe that the number of covered firms and the number of research reports would increase after analysts won the award, respectively. $ ^{23} $ These results support the argument that analysts have access to more resources after winning the award.

## 8 | Conclusion

Our study examines the effect of All-Star awards on analysts' choices and research quality. We find that All-Star award recipients have more accurate earnings forecasts than non-recipients after winning the award. We also examine the information content of their stock recommendations, as measured by 3-day abnormal returns. We find that for firms with higher information asymmetry, recommendations issued by All-Star analysts convey significantly greater information content than those of non-recipients. All-Star analysts are also less biased, and long-term abnormal returns following their buy recommendations are higher for the sample with only a sole analyst recommendation in the window. Additionally, All-Star analysts issue forecasts more frequently and focus on fewer industries while covering more firms within each industry, yet they maintain a similar total number of firms covered and forecasts issued. They also tend to lead larger research teams and conduct more site visits post award. These findings suggest that the All-Star award enhances analysts' performance, increases their incentives to reallocate effort, and improves their access to both internal and external resources. To enhance the understanding of the mechanism, we augment the archival tests with follow-up surveys of analysts and institutional investors. Our survey evidence indicates that site visits and roadshows increase after analysts win the award. Collectively, our results suggest that the award enhances performance by prompting a reallocation of effort and improving access to both external and internal resources.

Our paper contributes to the understanding of the consequences of All-Star awards by documenting that the All-Star awards improve the performance of recipients. Our findings thus shed light on the debate regarding whether All-Star analysts truly perform better than other analysts or whether the award is simply a popularity contest. An important implication of our findings is that the award indeed promotes better fundamental research in the profession of sell-side analysts; however, it is subject to a caveat. The conclusions are drawn from an RDD analysis based on Chinese All-Star analysts' voting data. China's institutional settings and culture differ from those of other countries, which may affect the generalizability of our conclusions. We leave it to future research to explore our research question in other settings.
